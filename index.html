My name is Daniel Howard. Friends also call me DHow ("Dee-How").

I am a graduate student Ph.D. candidate at the University of Notre Dame in the Department of Applied & Computational Mathematics & Statistics. In 2015, I graduated from the University of Wisconsin-Madison with a B.S. degree in Applied Mathematics, Engineering, & Physics.

My current research work focuses on computational fluid dynamics and numerical modeling of Indian monsoons utilizing High Performance Computing machinery alongside interactions between these climate dynamics and climate change. This work is under my advisors Prof. Diogo Bolster and Prof. David Richter with the Environmental Fluid Dynamics Group.

Alongside my interests in CFD and atmospheric dynamics, I'm also passionate about sustainability, energy, and environmental & social justice through groups like GreeND and Fossil Free ND. At Notre Dame, I founded the SIAM Student Chapter and ACMS GSO and am currently Co-President of the Science Policy Initiative. Lastly, I am an avid musician playing violin/French horn/singing in groups like the ND Symphony Orchestra and South Bend Chamber Singers.

Radial Basis Function Finite Difference Methods
RBF-FD Methods are an extension of spectral methods which solve partial differential equations but return to principles of standard finite difference methods for inspiration. The principle appeal of RBF-FD Methods is that they are meshless and easily scalable on supercomputing systems. In particle, they feature particular applications to geophysical flow problems, as can be found in climate modeling and numerical weather prediction.
